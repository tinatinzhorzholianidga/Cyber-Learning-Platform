# CyberHero — Topic Specification: Teachers & Parents + Cyber Guardians (Grades 8–12)

**Purpose:** Research-based topic list for the next two build targets. Hand this to Claude Code as the content spec.
**Basis:** Parent-question research (Childnet, UK Safer Internet Centre), Pew Research 2025 teen survey, APA Health Advisory on Adolescent Social Media Use, Child Mind Institute guidance, NCMEC/FBI sextortion data, Common Sense Media 9–12 curriculum, UNICEF Georgia.

---

## 1. What the research says (summary)

### What parents actually ask (recurring questions, verbatim themes)
1. "How much screen time is too much? How do I get them off the phone?" (the #1 question everywhere)
2. "Should I read my teen's messages / monitor their accounts, or is that spying?"
3. "My child knows more about tech than me — how can I possibly protect them?"
4. "How do I know if my child is being bullied / blackmailed / talking to a predator?"
5. "How do I even start the conversation without them shutting me down?"
6. "Should I let my underage child on TikTok/Instagram?" / "Is this game/app OK?"
7. "What do I do RIGHT NOW — something bad already happened?"

### What psychologists recommend (APA + Child Mind Institute)
- **Monitoring = scaffolding, not surveillance.** Oversight should decrease with age/maturity; goal is teens who can self-monitor. Ages 10–14: active review & coaching; 15–18: gradual autonomy. Depends on maturity, not just age.
- **Conversation over confrontation.** Open with empathy ("I get why you want to be online"), use "I noticed…" instead of accusations; keep dialogue non-judgmental so teens come to parents when things go wrong. This is THE core skill to teach parents.
- **Protect sleep and physical activity first** — 45% of teens say social media hurts their sleep (Pew 2025). Concrete rule-making (devices out of bedroom, no-phone hours) beats vague limits.
- **Watch for problem-use signs:** use that impairs daily roles/routines, mood changes, withdrawal.
- **Limit social-comparison & appearance content** — teen girls are measurably more affected (25% report mental-health harm vs 14% of boys).
- **Build media literacy early** — teens should understand how platforms hook them, algorithms, and fake content.
- **Teens themselves see the risk in others but not themselves** (48% say harms peers, 14% say harms self) → teen content must avoid preachiness; use scenarios, not lectures.

### The threat parents don't know to ask about
- **Financial sextortion is exploding** — reports up 300%+ (2021–2023, NCMEC); **teen boys are now the #1 target**. Offenders pose as girls, get an image in minutes, then demand money. Multiple teen suicides linked.
- Key safety messages (NCMEC): *the blackmailer is to blame, not you; never pay; block but don't delete (evidence); tell a trusted adult; "Take It Down" service can remove images.*
- This MUST be a first-class topic in both the teen track and the parent section.

### Georgian context (UNICEF Georgia + local guides)
- Same risk landscape; far less local material. UNICEF Georgia's advice mirrors the above: open communication, co-engagement, privacy settings, balance — validating our approach. Being THE Georgian-language resource is the gap we fill.

## 2. Teachers & Parents section — topics to build

Structure: seventh card on the welcome page → hub with three shelves: **Understand · Act · For School**. Tone: calm, practical, zero tech-jargon, never blaming the parent or the child.

### Shelf A — "Understand the risks" (explainers, 5-min reads, one per risk)
| # | Topic | Key content |
|---|-------|-------------|
| A1 | What your teen's online world looks like | Apps, gaming chats, DMs, algorithms; why "just ban it" fails; what teens say themselves (Pew data) |
| A2 | Phishing & scams that target kids | Free-skins/V-Bucks scams, fake giveaways, account-theft |
| A3 | Sextortion & blackmail (PRIORITY) | How it happens (fast, boys targeted), warning signs, the never-pay / don't-delete / not-your-fault script, Take It Down |
| A4 | Cyberbullying & digital drama | Signs a child is a target (or a bully), screenshot–block–report–support |
| A5 | Grooming & online strangers | How offenders build trust, moving platforms, red-flag phrases |
| A6 | Screen time, sleep & mental health | APA guidance; sleep first; problem-use warning signs; girls & social comparison |
| A7 | Fake news, deepfakes & dangerous trends | Media literacy for parents; viral challenges; AI-generated content |

### Shelf B — "Act" (tools, checklists, conversation kits)
| # | Topic | Key content |
|---|-------|-------------|
| B1 | How to talk so teens listen | Psychologist-backed scripts by age (8–12 vs 13–18): openers, "I noticed…", what NOT to say; keeping the door open |
| B2 | Monitor or trust? Finding the balance | Scaffolding model: more oversight young, earned autonomy later; agree rules WITH the teen; family media agreement template |
| B3 | Home setup in 30 minutes | Step-by-step: privacy settings, parental controls, router basics, device-free bedroom, family password rules |
| B4 | Warning signs checklist | Behavior changes that warrant a conversation (withdrawal, secrecy, night use, money asks, mood after phone use) |
| B5 | Emergency playbook: "It already happened" | If bullied / blackmailed / sextorted / account hacked / met a stranger: numbered steps, who to contact in Georgia, evidence preservation, what never to do |

### Shelf C — "For school" (teachers)
| # | Topic | Key content |
|---|-------|-------------|
| C1 | Classroom mode | Run any student mission on a projector; discussion questions per mission |
| C2 | Lesson plans & worksheets | Printable 45-min lesson per topic, matched to the 8–12 track |

## 3. Cyber Guardians (grades 8–12) — missions to build

Guide character: serious tone, minimal mascot. Format: scenario-first ("you get this DM — what do you do?"), never lectures (research: teens think risk applies to others, not them). Each mission = 4–6 interactive rounds + debrief.

| # | Mission | Covers | Game mechanic |
|---|---------|--------|---------------|
| G1 | Phishing Hunter | Phishing, social engineering, fake login pages, urgency tricks | Inbox simulator: real-or-phish on realistic emails/DMs/SMS |
| G2 | Footprint Detective | Digital footprint, reputation, what posts reveal; future jobs | Investigate a fictional profile like a stalker/recruiter would; then audit "your" profile |
| G3 | Fort Knox | Passwords, password manager, 2FA, account recovery | Build-your-defense simulator; crack-time visualizer |
| G4 | The Blackmail Trap (PRIORITY) | Sextortion & blackmail: how it starts, never pay, block don't delete, not your fault, Take It Down, tell someone | Branching story (choose-your-path) based on real NCMEC patterns; both boy & girl scenarios |
| G5 | Scam Radar | Money scams: fake shops, crypto/gift-card, game-item trades, fake jobs | Spot-the-scam speed round with escalating difficulty |
| G6 | Upstander | Cyberbullying, group-chat pile-ons, hate; screenshot–block–report; supporting targets | Group-chat simulator with intervention choices |
| G7 | Reality Check | Fake news, deepfakes, AI images, algorithm manipulation, confirmation bias | "Real or fake?" feed with reveal explanations |
| G8 | Stranger Signals | Grooming red flags, "move to another app", too-good-to-be-true friends/romance | DM conversation analyzer: flag the red-flag messages |
| G9 | Balance Boss | Sleep, doomscrolling, social comparison, problem-use self-check | Self-audit quiz + build-your-evening challenge (APA-based, non-preachy) |
| G10 | Final: Guardian Exam | All topics mixed | Timed mixed scenario gauntlet → Guardian certificate |

### Cross-linking (build this in)
Every parent explainer (A2–A7) links to the matching teen mission ("Have your teen play G4") and vice versa (mission debriefs: "worried? show the parents' guide"). This is the platform's unique loop: both sides learn the same case from their own seat.

## 4. Content rules
- Bilingual KA/EN from day one; Georgian reviewed by native team before ship.
- Sensitive topics (G4, G8, A3, A5): follow NCMEC victim-first framing — never blame, never shame, always name a next step and a trusted adult. No graphic content; fear is not the teaching tool, competence is.
- Local touches: Georgian bank-SMS scam formats, Georgian names in scenarios, Georgia-relevant contacts in the emergency playbook (112, MIA cyber division, school psychologist).
- Every stat used in content should carry its source (APA, Pew, NCMEC) in a small footnote — builds trust with teachers.
